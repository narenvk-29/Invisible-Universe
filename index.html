Black Holes - Cosmic Gravity Wells

What are Black Holes?
Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They form when massive stars (>25 solar masses) exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse under their own gravity.

Key Properties:

  • Event Horizon: The boundary beyond which escape is impossible. Radius = 2GM/c¬≤ (Schwarzschild radius)
  • Singularity: Central point of theoretically infinite density
  • No Hair Theorem: Black holes are characterized by only mass, spin, and charge
  • Hawking Radiation: Quantum effect causing black holes to slowly evaporate

Types of Black Holes:

  • Stellar: 3-100 solar masses, formed from collapsed stars
  • Supermassive: Millions to billions of solar masses, found in galaxy centers
  • Intermediate: 100-100,000 solar masses, rarely observed
  • Primordial: Hypothetical, may have formed in early universe

Historic Observations:

  • 2015: LIGO detects gravitational waves from merging black holes (Nobel Prize 2017)
  • 2019: Event Horizon Telescope captures first image of M87* black hole
  • 2020: Nobel Prize for discovery of Sgr A* at Milky Way center

Dark Matter - The Invisible Universe

What is Dark Matter?
Dark matter is a hypothetical form of matter that doesn't emit, absorb, or reflect light. It interacts only through gravity, making up 27% of the universe's total mass-energy content.

Evidence for Dark Matter:

  • Galaxy Rotation Curves: Galaxies rotate faster than visible matter can explain (Vera Rubin, 1970)
  • Gravitational Lensing: Light bends around invisible mass concentrations
  • Bullet Cluster: Direct proof - dark matter passed through collision while gas slowed
  • CMB Fluctuations: Precise measurements require dark matter to match observations
  • Galaxy Formation: Structures couldn't form fast enough without dark matter scaffolding

The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56):

  • Distance: 3.7 billion light-years from Earth
  • Collision Speed: 4,500 km/s (10 million mph)
  • What It Shows: Pink = hot X-ray gas (visible matter), Blue = dark matter (gravitational lensing)
  • Why It Matters: Dark matter and visible matter separated during collision - definitive proof

Dark Matter Candidates:

  • WIMPs: Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (10-1000 GeV/c¬≤)
  • Axions: Ultra-light particles (10‚Åª‚Å∂ to 10‚Åª¬≥ eV/c¬≤)
  • Sterile Neutrinos: Hypothetical heavy neutrinos (keV to GeV)
  • Primordial Black Holes: Black holes formed in early universe

The Big Bang - Origin of Everything

What is the Big Bang?
The Big Bang theory describes the universe's evolution from an extremely hot, dense initial state 13.8 billion years ago. Space itself expanded (and continues to expand), carrying matter and energy with it.

Key Epochs:

  • Planck Epoch (0-10‚Åª‚Å¥¬≥s): All forces unified, temperature >10¬≥¬≤ K
  • Inflation (10‚Åª¬≥‚Å∂s): Universe expands by factor of 10¬≤‚Å∂
  • Quark Epoch (10‚Åª¬π¬≤s): Quarks and gluons form quark-gluon plasma
  • Nucleosynthesis (3 min): First atomic nuclei form (75% H, 25% He)
  • Recombination (380,000 yrs): Atoms form, universe becomes transparent
  • Dark Ages (380,000-200M yrs): No stars yet, universe is dark
  • First Stars (200M yrs): Population III stars ignite

Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB):

  • What: The oldest light in the universe - released at recombination
  • Temperature: 2.72548 K (-270.4¬∞C)
  • Fluctuations: ¬±200 microKelvin (seeds of all cosmic structure)
  • Discovery: 1965 by Penzias & Wilson (Nobel Prize 1978)
  • Precision Mapping: COBE (1989), WMAP (2001), Planck (2009)

Evidence for the Big Bang:

  • CMB radiation matching predicted temperature
  • Abundance of light elements (H, He, Li) matches predictions
  • Hubble expansion - galaxies moving away from us
  • Large-scale structure formation matches simulations

Galaxy Clusters - Cosmic Laboratories

What are Galaxy Clusters?
Galaxy clusters are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe, containing hundreds to thousands of galaxies, vast amounts of hot gas, and enormous dark matter halos.

Composition:

  • Dark Matter: ~85% of total mass
  • Hot Gas (ICM): ~13% (50-100 million ¬∞C)
  • Galaxies: ~2% (stars, planets, dust)

Perseus Cluster (Abell 426):

  • Distance: 240 million light-years
  • Size: 863 galaxies confirmed
  • X-ray Luminosity: One of brightest in sky
  • Sound Waves: Chandra detected "sound" in hot gas (B‚ô≠, 57 octaves below middle C)
  • AGN Feedback: Central black hole creates ripples in gas

Why They Matter:

  • Laboratories for studying dark matter distribution
  • Test cosmological models
  • Reveal galaxy evolution in dense environments
  • X-ray observations show hot gas invisible to optical telescopes

Observation Methods:

  • X-ray: Hot gas (Chandra, XMM-Newton)
  • Optical: Galaxy distributions (Hubble, ground telescopes)
  • Gravitational Lensing: Dark matter mapping
  • Radio: Relativistic particles and magnetic fields
