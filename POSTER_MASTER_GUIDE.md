### 1. **Bullet Cluster - The Smoking Gun of Dark Matter**

![Bullet Cluster Composite](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/narenvk/Invisible-Universe/main/bullet_cluster_composite.jpg)

**ğŸ“ Poster Placement:** Top-left quadrant, featured as primary dark matter evidence

**ğŸ¯ Key Content for Poster:**
- **What it shows:** Two galaxy clusters colliding at 10 million mph
- **Pink regions:** Hot X-ray emitting gas (visible matter)
- **Blue regions:** Dark matter distribution mapped via gravitational lensing
- **Significance:** Dark matter passed through collision while gas slowed down - direct evidence of dark matter's existence

**ğŸ“Š Detailed Scientific Explanation:**

The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56) represents one of the most compelling pieces of evidence for dark matter's existence. This system consists of two colliding galaxy clusters located approximately 3.7 billion light-years from Earth in the constellation Carina.

**Collision Dynamics:**
- The smaller cluster (the "bullet") passed through the larger cluster at speeds exceeding 4,500 km/s
- During collision, the hot intracluster gas (comprising most of the visible matter) experienced drag forces and slowed down
- The galaxies themselves, being mostly empty space, passed through relatively unaffected
- Dark matter, being collisionless, passed straight through without interaction

**Observational Evidence:**
- **Chandra X-ray Observatory** mapped the hot gas (pink regions) showing clear separation from mass centers
- **Hubble Space Telescope** and ground-based telescopes measured weak gravitational lensing
- Lensing maps reveal mass distribution (blue regions) offset from visible matter by ~250,000 light-years
- Mass-to-light ratio indicates 85% of cluster mass is dark matter

**Why This Matters:**
This observation rules out alternative theories like MOND (Modified Newtonian Dynamics) because the gravitational center is clearly separated from the visible matter. The dark matter behaved exactly as predicted: collisionless and dominant in mass.

This NASA educational graphic illustrates the primary observational evidence for dark matter through multiple independent methods:

**1. Gravitational Lensing:**
- Light from distant galaxies bends around massive dark matter concentrations
- Creates Einstein rings, arcs, and multiple images
- Allows mapping of dark matter distribution without seeing it directly
- Weak lensing: statistical distortion of background galaxy shapes

- Stars in outer regions of galaxies orbit faster than expected from visible matter
- Flat rotation curves extend far beyond visible disk

- Galaxies in clusters move too fast to be held by visible matter alone
- Virial theorem analysis shows mass discrepancy
- Fritz Zwicky's original 1933 discovery in Coma Cluster
- Modern measurements confirm 80-90% of cluster mass is dark

**4. Cosmic Microwave Background:**
- CMB power spectrum reveals matter density
- Acoustic peaks indicate baryon-to-dark matter ratio
- Precisely measured by WMAP and Planck satellites
- Confirms ~85% of matter is non-baryonic (dark matter)

**Physics of Lensing:**
- Massive objects curve spacetime around them
- Light follows curved spacetime geodesics

**3. Microlensing:**
- Temporary brightening of background stars

This composite image showcases multiple galaxy clusters observed by NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory, demonstrating the diversity of these massive cosmic structures.

**Chandra X-ray Observatory:**

**Mission Details:**

**Why X-ray Observations Matter:**

**1. Hot Gas Detection:**
- Cluster gas heated to 10-100 million K by gravitational collapse
- Emits primarily in X-rays via thermal bremsstrahlung
- Optical telescopes can't see this component
- X-ray luminosity traces total mass

**2. Mass Measurement:**

**Cluster Diversity:**

**Relaxed Clusters:**
- Smooth, symmetric X-ray emission
- Peaked surface brightness
- Cool cores (central temperature drop)
- Examples: Perseus, Virgo, Hydra A
- Indicate no recent major mergers

**Merging Clusters:**
- Asymmetric, disturbed morphology
- Shock fronts and discontinuities
- Offset between gas and dark matter (e.g., Bullet Cluster)
- Examples: Bullet, Abell 520, MACS J0025
- Provide dark matter constraints

**Cool Core Clusters:**
- Central cooling time < Hubble time
- Should form stars at high rate (cooling flow problem)
- AGN feedback prevents runaway cooling
- ~70% of clusters have cool cores
- Examples: Perseus, Abell 1835, RX J1347

**Non-Cool Core Clusters:**
- Flat central temperature profile
- Recent merger disrupted cool core
- Lower central density
- Examples: Coma, Abell 2256
