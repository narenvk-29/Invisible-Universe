### Image 15: Cosmic Inflation Visualization

**What This Image Shows:**
A diagram illustrating the rapid, exponential expansion of the universe in its tiniest fraction of a second.

**Detailed Explanation:**

**The Timeline:**
- **Start:** $10^{-36}$ seconds after Big Bang
- **End:** $10^{-32}$ seconds after Big Bang
- **Duration:** Only $10^{-34}$ seconds!

**The Scale:**
- Before inflation: Smaller than a proton
- After inflation: Size of a grapefruit (or larger)
- **Expansion factor:** At least $10^{26}$ (equivalent to a pea growing to the size of the Milky Way!)

**Visual Elements:**
1.  **Tiny Point (Left):** Represents the observable universe before inflation. Highly curved, chaotic quantum fluctuations.
2.  **Rapid Expansion Cone (Middle):** Flared, trumpet-like shape showing explosive growth. Space expanding faster than light.
3.  **Smooth Universe (Right):** The result. A vast, flat, and uniform universe.

**Why It's Important:**
- **Solves the Horizon Problem:** Why is the CMB temperature the same everywhere? Because everything *was* connected in a tiny space before expanding apart.
- **Solves the Flatness Problem:** Why is the universe geometrically flat? Inflation stretched out any curvature, like inflating a balloon until its surface looks flat.
- **Origin of Structure:** Tiny quantum fluctuations (bumps) were stretched to cosmic sizes, becoming the seeds for galaxies (as seen in the CMB).

**For Your Poster:**
"Cosmic Inflation: In a fraction of a second, the universe expanded faster than light, stretching tiny quantum ripples into the seeds of giant galaxies."

### Image 16: Redshift Visualization

**What This Image Shows:**
A diagram explaining how the expansion of space stretches light waves, causing "redshift."

**Visual Elements:**
1.  **Emitter (Left):** A distant galaxy emitting light. Shows a blue/white wave with short wavelength ($\lambda_{emit}$).
2.  **Expanding Space (Middle):** A grid representing space-time. As the grid expands, the wave traveling through it is physically stretched.
3.  **Observer (Right):** Earth/Telescope receiving the light. The wave is now red with a long wavelength ($\lambda_{obs}$).

**The Physics:**
- **Cosmological Redshift is NOT Doppler Shift:** It's not caused by the galaxy moving *through* space (like a siren passing by). It's caused by space *itself* expanding and stretching the photon.
- **Formula:** $z = \frac{\lambda_{obs} - \lambda_{emit}}{\lambda_{emit}}$
- Higher $z$ = Further distance = Older time.

**Analogy:**
- Draw a wave on a rubber band.
- Stretch the rubber band.
- The wave gets longer (redder).

**For Your Poster:**
"Cosmic Redshift: As light travels through the expanding universe, space itself stretches the light waves, shifting them toward the red end of the spectrum."

## CATEGORY 3: OBSERVATIONAL EVIDENCE

### Image 17: Multi-Wavelength Astronomy (Crab Nebula Example)

**What This Image Shows:**
A composite or side-by-side comparison of the same object (e.g., Crab Nebula) seen in different wavelengths of light.

**The Wavelengths:**

1.  **Radio (Red):**
    - Reveals: Cold gas, electrons moving in magnetic fields (synchrotron radiation).
    - Telescope: VLA.
2.  **Infrared (Yellow/Orange):**
    - Reveals: Warm dust and forming stars. Penetrates dust clouds.
    - Telescope: Spitzer / James Webb.
3.  **Visible (Green):**
    - Reveals: Hot stars, ionized gas structures. What our eyes would see.
    - Telescope: Hubble.
4.  **X-ray (Blue/Purple):**
    - Reveals: Extremely hot gas, shock waves, particle winds from pulsar. High energy!
    - Telescope: Chandra.

**Relevance to Invisible Universe:**
- Most of the universe does *not* emit visible light.
- To understand the "full picture" (like the hot gas in Bullet Cluster), we **must** look at the invisible spectrum (X-rays, radio, IR).
- **The Invisible Universe is revealed by invisible light.**

**For Your Poster:**
"The Invisible Spectrum: To see the full universe, we must look beyond visible light. X-rays reveal hot gas, while infrared peers through dust, showing us what was previously hidden."

### Image 19: The Cosmic Distance Ladder

**What This Image Shows:**
A diagram illustrating the step-by-step methods astronomers use to measure vast distances in the universe.

**The Rungs of the Ladder:**
1.  **Parallax (Bottom):**
    - Method: Geometry/Triangulation.
    - Range: Nearby stars (up to few thousand light-years).
2.  **Cepheid Variables (Middle):**
    - Method: "Standard Candles." Pulsating stars where brightness relates to pulsation period.
    - Range: Nearby galaxies (up to ~100 million light-years).
    - *Key for Hubble's discovery!*
3.  **Type Ia Supernovae (Top):**
    - Method: Exploding white dwarfs. Always explode with same brightness.
    - Range: Distant universe (billions of light-years).
    - *Key for discovering Dark Energy!*

**Why it matters:**
- We can't use a ruler. We need overlapping methods to calibrate the next step.
- Any error in the bottom rung creates errors all the way up.

**For Your Poster:**
"Surveying the Cosmos: Astronomers use a 'ladder' of methods—from geometry to exploding stars—to measure the vast distances to other galaxies."

**What This Image Shows:**

**Visual Elements:**

- **The Grid:** A 3D wireframe grid representing space-time.

**For Your Poster:**
